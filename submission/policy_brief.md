# Policy Brief

## When the poor complement public services, fiscal policy becomes more powerful — and more redistributive

### The question

When the government increases spending, does the economy grow more if that spending goes to services used by poor households (public transit, public schools, Medicaid) rather than to services used by everyone equally (defense procurement, general infrastructure)? Macroeconomic textbooks treat government spending as a single aggregate number — one dollar of $G$ is one dollar of $G$, regardless of where it goes. This paper shows that the textbook picture misses a first-order margin: fiscal policy is substantially more effective, and more equitable, when it flows to services that low-income households use as inputs into their private consumption decisions.

### The mechanism in plain language

Low-income households use public services as complements to their own private spending. Public transit lowers commuting costs, making work feasible; public health services support nutrition investments; public schools support their children's education. When the government increases these services, the value of a dollar of their own income goes up — so they spend more, and the economy expands. Wealthy households, by contrast, often treat these services as substitutes for privately purchased alternatives (private schools, private healthcare, private security). For them, more public provision partly replaces what they were paying for themselves, and the stimulus is weaker.

When these two forces coexist in the same economy, the composition of fiscal spending matters. Spending that reaches the first group generates a multiplier well above 1. Spending that reaches the second group generates one near 1 or even below. Total fiscal effectiveness depends on how much of each goes where.

### What we find

Using US household data from 1996 to 2019 combined with state-year variation in Medicaid expansion, federal transit funding, and K–12 expenditure, we estimate how private consumption responds to public-good provision at different income levels. The pattern is sharp: bottom-quintile households show elasticities 3–5 times larger than top-quintile households, and with opposite signs in the categories that matter. Plugging these estimates into a standard macroeconomic model delivers impact multipliers of **1.70–1.84**, compared to a **1.15** benchmark that suppresses the complementarity channel. That is a **48–62% amplification**, almost half a percentage point of GDP per dollar of spending.

Three further results stand out:

**1. Targeting matters.** A dollar of fiscal expansion targeted at public health, transit, or education raises output by **24% more** than the same dollar of untargeted spending, while delivering a 1.8% consumption-equivalent welfare gain to the bottom quintile versus 0.2% to the top. The composition of fiscal policy is a more important lever than aggregate size.

**2. Austerity is more costly than standard models predict.** Cuts to public services reduce output **44% more** than separable models estimate, with welfare costs falling asymmetrically on the bottom of the distribution (−2.2% consumption for bottom quintile, −0.3% for top).

**3. The mechanism is strongest where it's most needed.** In developing economies with larger hand-to-mouth shares and sharper regressivity of public-good incidence ("the triple curse"), the impact multiplier reaches **2.64**. Well-targeted fiscal expansion in these countries does double duty: it stabilizes aggregate demand *and* delivers progressive distributional effects simultaneously.

### What this means for policy

- **Fiscal stimulus should be evaluated not just by size but by composition.** A recession-response package built around public-service maintenance is substantially more effective, per dollar, than a package of untargeted transfers.

- **Automatic stabilizers that preserve public-service provision during downturns earn a double dividend.** They stabilize aggregate demand *and* protect the private-consumption complementarity of the most vulnerable. Conventional cost–benefit analysis systematically under-estimates their value.

- **Austerity concentrated on public services is economically more contractionary and distributionally more regressive than standard analysis suggests.** Fiscal rules should incorporate differential treatment of spending categories, privileging those that generate high aggregate multipliers.

- **In developing economies, scaling up public services is simultaneously a stabilization tool and a poverty-reduction tool.** The framework provides a micro-founded rationale for the IMF's recent revisions to its multiplier estimates for low-income countries, and strengthens the case for protecting social expenditure in consolidation programs.

### Bottom line

The distributional incidence of public services is not a separate question from macroeconomic stabilization — it *is* macroeconomic stabilization. Recognizing this reframes fiscal policy as a question of distributional design, not aggregate size.
